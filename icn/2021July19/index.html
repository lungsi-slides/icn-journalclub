About Heart Failure

General circulation scheme

What is heart failure?

"Clinical syndrome that develops when the heart cannot maintain adequate output, or can do so only at the expense of elevated ventricular filling pressure." (Ralston et al., 2018)

Heart failure occurs when cardiac output fails to meet the demands of the circulation.

Ventricular dysfunction: The most common cause of heart failure

  • This can occur as imparied systolic contraction or diastolic dysfunction.
  • Systolic and diastolic dysfunctions often coexist.
  • Commonly found in hearts with left ventricular hypertrophy.

Left ventricular hypertrophy

Cardiac Electrophysiology

Atria to Ventricle Conduction

Electrical conduction starts from the atria. This is followed by the generation of action potential in the ventricles.

Characteristic action potential from ventricular muscles

The cardiac ventricular muscle fiber comprise many myofibrils. The ion channels of the ventricular muscle cell membrane called the sarcolemma is responsible for the shape of the action potential.

Four phases of action potential in ventricular muscle

At resting state K+ diffuses out of the cell. The shape of the action potential begin with phase-0 which is due to the opening of fast Na+ channel resulting in Na+ influx. The fast Na+ channel closes and K+ influx from the opened fast K+ channel (that began in phase-0) results in phase-1. The fast K+ channel closes and Ca2+ influx from the fully open slow Ca2+ channel (slowly opening during phases-0 & 1) causes phase-2. The slow Ca2+ channel closes and the outflow of K+ from the fully open slow K+ channel (slowly opening during previous phases) causes phase-3. As the slow K+ channel closes and K+ only diffuses resulting in phase-4 of the action potential.

Action potential in ventricular muscle spread into T-tubules

The spread of ventricular muscle action potential into the T-tubules triggers a cascade of steps for ventricular muscle contraction and relaxation.

Action potential in T-tubules triggers Ca2+ cascade

Purkinje fibers activates action potential from ventricular muscles. The action potential spreads into the T-tubules. This causes opening of L-type Ca channels resulting in Ca2+ influx. The calcium influx triggers opening of the ryanodine receptor causing Ca2+ sparks. The increased cytoplasmic Ca2+ causes muscle contraction. After the ventricular muscle has contracted the cytoplasmic Ca2+ is stored via the SERCA and extruded via the NCX. The excess Na+ in the cytoplasm is expelled by the Na-K-ATPase. Finally, the resting state is attained.

Authors question

Facts:

  • The amount of calcium removed from the cytoplasm is equivalent to the the amount entering the cell during early action potential - calcium entering via L-type channel triggering calcium sparks.
  • The calcium entering the cell is coupled with sodium entering the cell mediated by the Na-K-ATPase.
  • Muscle contractility is decreased in hypertrophic heart.

Hypothesis

Increased cytoplasmic concentration of sodium leads to increased cytoplasmic calcium. This mechanism compensates for the reduced contractility of a hypertrophic heart.

The question

What is the function of Na-K-ATPase in a hypertrophic heart?

Inducing Hypertrophy in Animal heart

By constricting the arch of the aorta the workload on the left ventricle is increased. With the constriction in place the left ventricle muscle eventually becomes hypertrophied.

Results part-A

in vivo assessment: As ventricular hypertrophy progresses how does it affect the cardiac function?

Precursor of ventricular dysfunction & heart failure: As the hypertrophy progresses the fractional shortening of the left ventricle (FS = ((diastolic internal dimension) − (systolic internal dimension)) ÷ (systolic internal dimension)) becomes smaller. The shortening is significant at 150 days post-AC.
Progression of cardiac hypertrophy: The heart-to-body weight ratio increases significantly at 60 and 150 days post-AC. Hypertrophy develops around 60 days and maintained thereafter. The lung-to-body weight ratio increases significantly at 150 days post-AC. Although, hypertrophy has occurred there is no decrease in heart function between 30 to 60 days post-AC. The heart begins to fail at 150 days post-AC. At 60 days post-AC the hypertrophic heart is considered to be in compensated stage.

As hypertrophy porgresses what does the kinetic of cytoplasmic Ca2+ look like?

Ca2+ transient: Is it different? How is it different? What does it mean?
Compared to control the amplitude of Ca2+ at compensated stage is larger. But, as the hypertrophy progresses the amplitude decreases significantly. Decrease in amplitude indicates slow removal of cytosol Ca2+.

Are any transporter responsible for the slow removal of cytosol Ca2+?

Looking at the time constants for SERCA, Na-Ca exchanger (NCX) and other miscellaneous transporters. At compensated stage the time constants remain unchanged. However, as the hypertrophy progresses, at 150 days the time constant for SERCA makes a significant decline Thus, suggesting reduced rate of removing cytosol Ca2+ by SERCA. Also, the Ca2+ content in the sarcoplasmic reticulum is significantly reduced at 150 days post-AC. The consequence of these two reasons is the rise in cytosol Ca2+ in severe hypertrophy.

What about Ca2+ sparks?

Ca2+ sparks initiate cardiac muscle contraction but it also plays a role in maintaining cytosol Ca2+ concentration. The spark frequency increases at 150 AC. This implies increased Ca2+ SR stores therefore resulting in increased Ca2+ cytosol. Multiplying the amount of spark frequency by the amount of spark mass the authors conlcuded increased spark mediated SR leak.

What could explain the results seen during compensated stage of hypertrophy?

The increased positive inotrophy, the high Ca2+ transient amplitude and the increased sarcoplasmic reticulum Ca2+ content.

Authors hypothesis

A possible explanation for the results at compensated stage could be due to inhibition of the Na-K-ATPase. Inhibiting the pump will cause increased cytoplasmic Na+. The consequence of raised cytoplasmic Na+ concentration is decreased expulsion of cytoplasmic Ca2+ thus raising the cytoplasmic Ca2+ content. It will then follow that the SR content of Ca2+ will rise.

Results part-B: Assessing the role of Na-K-ATPase

With progressive hypertrophy how does the isoform of the pump affect current through it?

Inhibiting specific isoform (α-2) or total inhibition of the Na-K-ATPase protein and measuring the current through it we observe no change in current as the hypertrophy progresses.

What is the pump activity after reactivation?

The Na-K-ATPase when active expells 3Na+ in exchange for 2K+ inflow. Thus, the net current is 1Na+ outflow. The pump activity can therefore be described by the rate of decay of the Na-K-ATPase current. When the cardiac hypertrophy progresses at 150AC the rate of decay is slower, that is, low rate of expelling cytosol Na+. If the area under the curve is a measure of the amount of extruded Na+, then the area for 150AC is smaller. Hence, the amount of extruded Na+ is decreased in hypertrophy.
What could explain for the decreased Na-K-ATPase current and the decreased Na+ outflow?

Is the amount of pump protein decreased during hypertrophy?

As the hypertrophy progresses to 150AC the fluorescence for α-1 & α-2 isoforms are decreased. Furthermore, Western blotting showed lowered levels of α-1 isoform (but not α-2 isoform) and lowered levels of the pump regulator phospholemman (PLM) — the PLM decline is lesser at 150AC.

Could the 50% reduction in pump current during compensated stage be a mechanism for preserving contractility function?

When the Na-K-ATPase current is reduced we observe increase in Ca2+ transient amplitude. The Na+/Ca2+ exchange current is also increased.
Hypothesis: During compensated stage the inhibition of Na-K-ATPase current increases SR load (Ca2+ SR store) and consequently maintains Ca2+ transient.

How does SERCA affect during the compensated stage?

If 50% reduction in Na-K-ATPase current causes increased Ca2+ transients leading to positive inotrophy during compensated stage, what would happen if the SERCA is partially inhibited? The Ca2+ transient is reduced. Under this condition what if the Na+ influx is increased? This does not augment Ca2+ transient.

Do the action potential change during hypertrophy?

The duration of action potential increases as the hypertrophy progresses. It has been suggested that in addition to the fast Na+ channel there exist another Na+ influx during action potential called late Na+ current that contributes to the duration of phase-2 plateau of the action potential. When the late Na+ current is inhibited, the duration of action potential of a hypertrophic heart is shortened. As the hypertrophy progresses the late Na+ current increases. This supports the suggestion that in a failing heart the density of late Na+ current increases and/or its inactivation kinetics is slowed.
